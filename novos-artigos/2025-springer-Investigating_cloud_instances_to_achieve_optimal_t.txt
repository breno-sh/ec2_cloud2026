Abstract
The growing demand for data-intensive parallel applications in cloud computing
requires a cost-effective approach to utilizing distributed processing power. However, due to the various instance types and configurations available, choosing the
right instance type and configuration to execute any given workload is essential to
maximize workload performance while minimizing costs. In this context, our comprehensive evaluation of 104 instances across three major cloud providers for eighteen parallel workloads reveals that no single instance excels in all metrics. While
high-performance computing (HPC) optimized instances can improve performance
by up to 66% for scaling workloads, they are not always cost-effective. In contrast, general-purpose instances provide a better performance-cost balance for less
demanding tasks. Therefore, we show that selecting the most suitable instance based
on the workload characteristics can lead to a 35.7% performance improvement and
a 6.5% cost reduction compared to using only one instance type for all workloads.

1 Introduction
Emerging data-intensive applications, such as machine learning and data mining,
increasingly rely on the processing power of cloud computing to execute highperformance computing (HPC) tasks. However, while the diverse range of cloud
instance types and configurations provides flexibility, it also introduces significant
cost and performance optimization challenges. This is further complicated by the
diminishing improvements in efficiency with each new generation of nodes, a phenomenon known as the end of Dennard scaling. As a result, selecting the most suitable instance type for specific workloads is critical to balancing cost and performance
effectively.
Optimizing the cost-performance trade-off for parallel workloads in the cloud
presents two key challenges. First, major cloud providers like Amazon Web Services
(AWS), Google Cloud Platform (GCP), and Microsoft Azure offer a wide variety of
instance types, including general-purpose, compute-optimized, and HPC-optimized
instances, each tailored to specific workload demands [1, 2]. The diversity among
these options and varying price models complicates the decision-making process.
Second, parallel workloads, which fully utilize hardware resources such as cores and
cache memory, often face performance bottlenecks due to hardware and software
factors like bus saturation, memory contention, and synchronization overhead [3].
Consequently, increasing the number of cores does not guarantee proportional performance improvements, as workload characteristics and memory access patterns
interact differently according to the instance type. This variability makes identifying
a single optimal instance for all workloads challenging.
While previous studies have examined the potential of cloud computing for
executing parallel workloads and proposed optimization strategies, as discussed in
Sect. 2.3, this work advances the state-of-the-art by presenting an extensive evaluation of performance and cost across a diverse set of cloud instances. Unlike prior
studies, we analyze how the scalability of parallel workloads is influenced by their
inherent characteristics, impacting the selection of the most suitable cloud instance.
Therefore, existing research can use our findings to further refine strategies for automatically identifying optimal instances for executing parallel workloads. To this
end, we conduct a comprehensive cost-performance evaluation of eighteen parallel
workloads with varying memory and CPU demands executed across 104 instances
from three leading cloud providers – GCP, AWS, and Microsoft Azure. Our key
findings include:
• No single instance type achieves the best balance of cost and performance across

all parallel workloads.

• HPC-optimized instances deliver up to 66% performance gains for scalable work-

loads but are not always cost-efficient compared to general-purpose instances.

• General-purpose instances provide a more balanced cost-performance ratio for

less demanding tasks.

2 Background
In this section, we provide background regarding Cloud Computing and the bottlenecks that affect thread scalability. Then, we discuss the related work and highlight
the contributions of our work.
2.1 Cloud computing
Cloud Computing has become the standard for running applications, offering ondemand resources over the Internet [4]. However, early on, challenges arose in
meeting the needs of compute-intensive applications, such as Big Data and Analytics, which require fast response times. These challenges were primarily due to
the overhead of hypervisors, despite technologies designed to ensure elasticity and
high availability [5]. The impact of virtualization and containerization on workload
performance and user costs is also significant in cloud environments. These technologies introduce an additional layer of abstraction, which can affect both computational efficiency and cost structure [6].
Bare-metal instances run directly on physical hardware and offer the highest
performance due to the lack of virtualization or containerization overhead. On the
other hand, containers and VMs introduce additional abstraction layers that share
underlying physical resources, potentially reducing performance. However, containerization generally introduces less overhead than VMs, as containers share the host
OS kernel, making them more efficient. Containers running within VMs experience
compounded overhead due to the added virtualization layer, which may negatively
impact performance, especially for resource-intensive workloads that require low
latency and fine-grained resource allocation.
From a cost perspective, bare-metal instances are typically more expensive due
to their dedicated resources, which are justified by the need for high performance
in specific applications. VMs and containers, on the other hand, tend to offer more
cost-effective solutions for less resource-intensive workloads, as they allow for
higher resource utilization. Containers, in particular, are known for their cost-efficiency in cloud environments, as they enable more workloads to run on the same
infrastructure with minimal overhead. As a result, lightweight container technologies like Docker gained popularity, as they deliver performance close to non-virtualized systems. Docker has emerged as the leading framework for developing,

Docker is particularly well-suited for deploying parallel workloads in cloud environments. It provides isolated environments with the required dependencies, and its

run applications efficiently, regardless of the underlying infrastructure.
Cloud computing offers various instance types tailored to different workloads,
such as general-purpose, compute-optimized, and HPC-optimized instances. General-purpose instances (e.g., AWS M-series, GCP c3-series) provide a balanced
resource mix, making them versatile but less suited for specialized tasks. Compute-optimized instances (e.g., AWS C-series) deliver better performance for CPUintensive workloads but come at a higher cost. HPC-optimized instances (e.g., AWS
H-series) are designed for scientific simulations and large-scale data analysis, providing advanced architectures at a premium price.
2.2 Thread scalability
When executing parallel workloads in cloud environments, software developers
usually deploy the job using the maximum available resources, such as cores and
cache memory. However, different studies have shown that this approach may not
yield the best performance [3, 7]. The reasons for this are related to both software
and hardware issues, including off-chip bus saturation, concurrent shared memory
accesses, and data-synchronization. Off-chip Bus Saturation Large applications with
significant amounts of private data stored in main memory and requiring frequent
access may encounter scalability issues when the connection between the memory
and processor becomes overloaded. Hence, as more threads are added, the demand
for the connection increases linearly, but its bandwidth is limited by I/O pins [8] and
does not increase along with the number of threads. Adding more threads will not
enhance performance when the bandwidth reaches its limits and may only lead to
increased power consumption.
Concurrent shared memory accesses Similarly, the workload performance can
be impacted by the amount of access to shared memory addresses. This is because
threads communicate by accessing shared data in areas that are typically distant
from the core, such as the last-level cache and main memory. As a result, this communication has higher latency per access than private caches, potentially creating
bottlenecks affecting the behavior of parallel workloads [7]. Data-synchronization
Also, there are scenarios where multiple threads must communicate by accessing
the same variable addresses. To prevent conflicts and ensure the accuracy of the
result, synchronization directives are employed. However, when multiple threads
reach a synchronization point, only one can execute it simultaneously, causing a portion of the workload to be serialized. As the number of threads increases, this leads

2.3 Related work
The exploration of parallel computing in cloud environments has garnered attention
in recent research. Ekanayake et al. [9] evaluate using cloud technologies for parallel
processing in scientific workloads. Rak et al. [10] propose a method to predict application costs across various execution scenarios and cloud providers using benchmarking and simulation techniques. Rathnayake et al. [11] introduce CELIA, an
analytical model that identifies cost-effective cloud configurations that balance time
and expense. Wan et al. [12] developed a cost-performance model for cloud services
through a queuing system that simulates dynamic resource allocation.
Zhang et al. [13] present the EPRD algorithm, which aims to minimize task
scheduling duration for parallel tasks with precedence constraints. Li et al. [14]
introduce the AdaptFR strategy to expedite the training of distributed machine
learning models. Ohue et al. [15] investigate using cloud platforms for protein-protein docking simulations. Bhardwaj et al. [16] address task scheduling on parallel
machines with precedence constraints in cloud computing environments. Haussmann et al. [17] propose a novel cost model that quantifies the financial implications of parallel computing using a combination of reserved and volatile processors.
Furthermore, Haussmann et al. [18] explore irregularly structured problems in parallel applications, presenting a method to determine the optimal number of processors based on input data variability. Haussmann et al. [19] also propose an elasticity
description language and mechanisms to facilitate efficient resource allocation for
parallel applications.
Gillam et al., [20] consider different parallel workloads running on four instance
types to discuss how to measure and use quality of service (QoS) information to predict the behavior of cloud servers regarding availability, risk, and liability in case of
failure. Khan et al., [21] focus on reducing data center energy consumption through
efficient scheduling and resource management while maintaining workload performance despite resource heterogeneity. It proposes a reference architecture with a
unified resource manager, eliminating the need for dedicated services like schedulers or monitors for different environments. Simulations on the CloudSim using real
cloud workloads were done to validate the proposed approach. Zakarya and Gillam
[22] investigate migration strategies to improve virtual machine (VM) performance
while achieving the expected performance levels at agreed pay-per-use costs. It also
evaluates the impact of various VM allocation policies and migration approaches on
energy efficiency, performance, and cost. All the evaluations were performed on the
CloudSim infrastructure.
O’Loughlin and Gillam [23] propose a cloud service broker (CSB) that offers
a secondary market for on-demand, workload-specific performance-assurance services. For that, users specify a performance tranche, defining a minimum performance level for their workload, and instances are re-priced accordingly. Also,
it focuses on estimating the CSB’s operational costs and the expected number of

3 Methodology
In this section, we depict the target instances and the parallel workloads used during
the experiments.
3.1 Target instances
We consider a diverse set of 104 cloud instances categorized into two groups: fiftytwo from the general-purpose domain (Table 2) and fifty-two from the computeand HPC-optimized domains (Table 3. The general-purpose domain includes cloud
instances designed for versatility, providing a balanced mix of compute, memory,
and network resources. These instances, like AWS m7g, GCP n2, and Azure Dv5
series, are cost-effective for tasks with moderate resource demands but may lack
the performance needed for highly specialized workloads. On the other hand, the
compute- and HPC-optimized domain features instances tailored for compute- and
memory-intensive tasks such as scientific simulations, machine learning, and largescale data analytics. Examples include AWS hpc7g, GCP h3, and Azure HBv3 series,
offering high core counts and large memory capacities. Moreover, the selected
instances feature a variety of processor architectures, including AMD EPYC, Intel
Xeon, and ARM-based AWS Graviton processors, to provide a broad perspective on
performance and cost efficiency across hardware platforms.
We included multiple configurations with varying numbers of virtual CPUs
(vCPUs) for each instance type to evaluate scalability. For example, within the GCP
n2 and n2 series, we selected instances with 8, 16, 32, 64, and 128 vCPUs, enabling us to assess how efficiently parallel applications leverage additional cores and
memory bandwidth. Similarly, configurations such as 16, 32, 64, and 128 vCPUs
were analyzed in the compute- and HPC-optimized domains to capture the scaling
behavior for scaling workloads. All instances were configured with the same Operating System (Ubuntu 22.04) and kernel version to ensure consistency and avoid
variability in the experiments. For cost analysis, the on-demand pricing model was
chosen across all cloud providers (GCP, AWS, and Microsoft Azure). This model

4 Experimental evaluation
This section discusses the results of executing each parallel workload on the target instances. For that, we have split the discussion into the following subsections:
(i) in Sect. 4.1, we evaluate the behavior of the general-purpose instances; (ii) in
Sect. 4.2, we discuss the results on the compute- and HPC-optimized instances; (iii)
in Sect. 4.4, we compare the best results achieved on each type of instance. Finally,
Sect. 4.5 lists the limitations of this study.
In the next subsections, we consider three different metrics for evaluation:
• Execution Time reports the total time taken for the workload execution on each

instance. It was measured using the omp_get_wtime function from the
OpenMP API.
• Cost quantifies the monetary expense required to execute a specific workload on
a cloud instance. We calculated it based on the cloud provider’s on-demand pricing model, dividing the instance’s hourly rate by the total execution time for the
workload.
• Cost-Performance Balance evaluates the trade-off between minimizing cost and
maximizing performance (execution time). This balance is computed using the
Euclidean Distance between the origin (0, 0) and a data point (Cost, Execution
Time) on a Cartesian plane. Therefore, the closer the point is to the origin, the
better the balance between cost and performance.
The process for running each workload on the cloud instance was as follows. First,
workloads were executed within Docker containers to ensure portability and isolated
execution. Each container included all necessary dependencies and was deployed on
the target instances. Second, the number of virtual CPUs (vCPUs) assigned to the
container matches the total vCPUs available on the respective instances, as this represents the standard way parallel workloads are executed. Each workload-instance
pair was executed 30 times, and the results presented in the next subsections consider the average results with a standard deviation of less than 0.5%.

4.1 General‑purpose instances evaluation
We begin by analyzing the results of running each workload on general-purpose
instances. Figure 3a displays the relationship between performance (y-axis) and cost
(x-axis) for executing the entire set of parallel workloads on each target instance. In
this plot, points that are closer to the origin (0,0) indicate a better balance between
performance and cost. When all workloads are executed on a single instance, the
AWS m7g.12xlarge instance delivered the best performance results, while the AWS

4.3 The impact of cloud instance on the thread scalability
In this subsection, we discuss the behavior of the parallel workloads on the cloud
instances according to the thread scalability issues described in Sect. 2. Let us
start by discussing the behavior of workloads with thread scalability limited by the
amount of data synchronization operations, such as BFS, where the best performance and cost were achieved with instances with only 8 vCPus. In this type of
workload, when the number of running threads increases, the synchronization time
also increases, worsening performance as more threads run concurrently [34].
For applications that handle large amounts of private data and require consistent
access from the main memory (e.g., FFT and LL), there is a point at which off-chip
bus saturation becomes critical, as discussed in Sect. 2. In this scenario, the offchip bus usage significantly impacts the scalability of the workload threads. Since
each thread operates on different data blocks, the demand for off-chip bus bandwidth
increases linearly with the number of threads (and vCPUs). However, due to the
limitation of the bus’s I/O pins, the bandwidth does not increase proportionately to
the number of processor cores (vCPUs). As a result, when the off-chip bus becomes
saturated, increasing the number of threads does not lead to further performance
improvements. With that, the best performance of the FFT workload was reached
by exploiting parallelism with 16 vCPUs on the general-purpose (m7g.4xlarge)
and HPC-optimized (hpc7g.4xlarge) instances. Beyond this point, increasing the
number of vCPUs to the maximum available on the instances resulted in 5.6%
increase in execution time for the general-purpose scenario (compared to 64 vCPUs
– m7g.16xlarge) and 5.01% increase for the HPC-Optimized scenario (compared to
64 vCPUs – hpc7g.16xlarge).

4.5 Limitations and opportunities for improvements
This research evaluates the performance and cost-effectiveness of cloud instances
from various providers through experiments. However, there are some limitations. First, our analysis is constrained by the availability of cloud resource
quotas, preventing the inclusion of more general-purpose, HPC, and computeoptimized instances from other providers. This limits our comparative analysis.
The study focuses solely on CPU-based instances and does not address GPU
instances, which are often beneficial for modern HPC workloads due to their parallel processing capabilities. Additionally, the parallel workloads were considered
to run on a single machine, so we did not analyze network performance variability between instances and providers, which could affect distributed workloads.
Another limitation is that it does not directly investigate the interference of colocated workloads, which can significantly impact both performance and cost efficiency in cloud environments. Colocation of workloads can introduce resource
contention, leading to performance degradation and increased costs, particularly
when workloads are memory- or compute-intensive. Our cost analysis is based
on standard on-demand pricing. We excluded alternative pricing models like
reserved and spot instances to maintain consistency and comparability across
instance types and providers. While these alternatives can offer cost savings, they
introduce uncertainties and variability that complicate the analysis. Focusing on
on-demand pricing ensures our cost analysis remains straightforward and easily
comparable across all evaluated instances.

5 Conclusion and future work
This study investigates the optimization of performance and cost for parallel
workloads in the cloud across a range of instance types from major providers,
including Amazon Web Services, Google Cloud Platform, and Microsoft Azure.
Our experiments with eighteen parallel workloads across 104 cloud instances
revealed that no single instance type offers the best balance of performance and
cost for all workloads. Compute- and HPC-optimized instances showed significant performance improvements, with some tasks achieving up to 66% better performance, making them ideal for high-demand computational tasks. HPC-optimized instances were also the most cost-effective, offering a 14.8% lower cost
than general-purpose instances. Meanwhile, general-purpose instances offered a
better balance for memory-intensive or moderately demanding workloads, outperforming HPC-optimized instances in scenarios that did not require extreme performance. Future research will extend this analysis to GPU-based instances and
explore various pricing models, such as reserved and spot instances, to understand cost dynamics better and further optimize performance and cost efficiency.
